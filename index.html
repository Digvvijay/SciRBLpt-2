Biodiesel production involves these steps:



1. Feedstock Preparation: Collect and refine vegetable oils or animal fats.

2. Transesterification: React oils/fats with alcohol (usually methanol) and a catalyst

(often sodium hydroxide) to form biodiesel and glycerin.

3. Separation: Let the mixture settle, separating biodiesel (upper layer) from glycerin
(lower layer).

4. Washing: Clean biodiesel by removing excess alcohol, catalyst, and impurities.

5. Drying: Remove any remaining water from biodiesel.

6. Quality Testing: Analyze biodiesel for compliance with standards.

7. Final Product: Obtain biodiesel ready for use as an alternative fuel.




Raw Materials Required for Biodiesel Production:



1. Vegetable oils

2. Animal fats

3. Methanol

4. A catalyst (usually sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide).




Treatment of Raw Materials Required in Biodiesel Production:



1. Feedstock Refinement: Clean and filter vegetable oils or animal fats to remove impurities.

2. Methanol Purification: Ensure methanol is free of water and impurities.

3. Catalyst Handling: Prepare and dissolve the catalyst (sodium/potassium hydroxide) in methanol solution.

4. Alcohol and Catalyst Mixing: Combine methanol-catalyst mixture with feedstock for reaction.




Purification of Biodiesel:



1. Washing: Biodiesel is washed with water to remove residual impurities and excess catalyst.

2. Drying: Water is separated from biodiesel through settling or centrifugation.

3. Quality Check: Test biodiesel for adherence to specifications before use.




Advantages of Biodiesel:



1. Renewable

2. Reduces greenhouse gases

3. Compatible with existing diesel engines




Disadvantages of Biodesel:



1. Variable quality

2. Potential land competition

3. Impact on food prices




Uses of Biodesel:



1. Transportation fuel (diesel engines)

2. Heating fuel

3. Power generation
